Sensory Processing Differences
Also known as Sensory Processing Disorder (SPD) or Sensory Integration Difficulties
NeurodevelopmentalCommonly associated with autism, ADHD and dyspraxiaEquality Act 2010 may apply
What Sensory Processing Differences actually is

Sensory processing differences describe how the brain receives, interprets and responds to sensory information from the environment and the body. For someone with sensory processing differences, sensory input that others barely notice may be experienced as intense, distracting, uncomfortable or even physically painful. Sensory differences can run in two directions — hypersensitivity (over-responsive) and hyposensitivity (under-responsive, seeking more input). They are strongly associated with autism, ADHD and dyspraxia — but can also occur independently.

Sensory sensitivities are just personal preferences or fussiness
Sensory processing differences are neurological, not attitudinal. The discomfort or distress experienced is real and often significant.
People should just get used to it over time
For many people with significant sensory differences, repeated exposure to overwhelming input leads to cumulative fatigue rather than adaptation.
Only autistic people have sensory processing differences
While sensory differences are very common in autism, they occur across a wide range of neurodivergent conditions and can exist without any other diagnosis.
How it might show up at work
What you might observe → what it may actually mean
Wears headphones constantly, even when not on a call→Managing auditory overload — headphones are a legitimate sensory regulation tool, not rudeness or disengagement
Avoids the open-plan area, canteen or busy social spaces→Sensory-rich environments are genuinely overwhelming — avoidance is self-regulation, not antisocial behaviour
Seems irritable, distracted or fatigued after busy periods→Sensory overload is physically and cognitively exhausting — what looks like mood or attitude is often neurological depletion
Raises complaints about temperature, lighting or smell that seem minor→These are not minor to someone with sensory differences — they are genuine barriers to functioning that warrant a practical response
Moves around a lot, fidgets or seeks physical stimulation→Hyposensitivity — seeking sensory input to reach a comfortable level of stimulation. Movement aids regulation and focus, not distraction
Appears distressed by unexpected touch — a pat on the back, a handshake→Tactile hypersensitivity — physical contact that feels neutral or friendly to most people can be genuinely painful or distressing
Struggles to concentrate in open-plan spaces despite apparent capability→Sensory filtering takes cognitive resources — when those are consumed managing the environment, less is available for the work itself
What it might feel like for the individual

"Imagine arriving at work and the lights are slightly too bright, the air conditioning is just loud enough that you can't quite ignore it, someone nearby is eating something pungent and the open-plan hum of twenty conversations is coming at you from every direction. You sit down to do some focused work. But your brain isn't processing the spreadsheet in front of you — it's processing all of that, all at once, all the time. The spreadsheet gets whatever's left."

"By 11am you're tired in a way that's hard to explain. By 2pm you're managing yourself as much as you're managing the work. You've learned to hide this because the one time you mentioned the lights, someone said 'really? I hadn't noticed.' So now you just get on with it."

Sensory overload isn't a momentary discomfort — it's a cumulative drain that builds across the day. Many people with sensory processing differences arrive at work already managing their environment before they've opened a single email.

Because the sensory environment that is overwhelming to one person feels entirely normal to others nearby, there is often a painful disconnect — a sense of being unreasonable, dramatic or difficult for struggling with something that no one else seems to notice.

Reasonable adjustments

These are starting points, not prescriptions. Always ask the individual what would actually help them.

EasyAllow use of noise-cancelling headphones at all times — explicitly accepted, not just tolerated. Make it a standard working practice, not an exception.
EasyAllow flexible seating choices — away from high-traffic areas, near windows for natural light, or in quieter zones.
EasyEstablish a no-fragrance norm in the immediate team environment where possible, or ensure access to a fragrance-free workspace.
EasyAllow regular movement breaks — framing these as normal rather than exceptional removes the need for anyone to explain themselves.
Some planningOffer hybrid or remote working where the role allows — working from home removes the accumulated sensory load of the office environment and can dramatically improve both output and wellbeing.
Some planningReview lighting in the individual's work area — where possible, allow natural light, reduce fluorescent overhead lighting, or allow use of a desk lamp or anti-glare filter.
Some planningProvide advance notice of any environmental changes — office moves, refurbishments, changes to seating. What feels administrative to most people can be significantly destabilising for someone whose coping strategies are built around a familiar environment.
Refer to specialistAccess to Work can fund noise-cancelling headphones, specialist lighting, ergonomic workstation equipment and sensory-specific workplace assessments.

Speaking to your team
🔒

Before speaking with the individual, don't signal to the team that a conversation is happening. Begin introducing inclusive habits as team-wide standards — this creates the right environment before any individual conversation takes place.

After agreeing adjustments, implement them in a way that doesn't draw attention to one person. Frame changes as team-wide improvements.

"I've been thinking about how we work as a team and I'm making a few changes that I think will help us all — around how we communicate, how we structure tasks and how we support each other."

"I never discuss individual team members with colleagues — that's just how I work. These changes are about how we all work together."

"Is there anything about how we communicate or work together that would help you personally?"

Supporting the wider team

A team environment that works well for neurodivergent people tends to work better for everyone. This section covers team culture shifts and guidance on supporting someone without breaching confidentiality.

🔊
Normalise noise managementMake headphone use, quiet zones and away-from-desk working entirely normal and unremarkable. When managing sensory input is a standard practice, no one has to explain or justify it.
🏛
Create genuine seating flexibilityWhere possible, give people real choice about where they work — near windows, in quieter areas, away from high-traffic routes. Fixed desk allocation in sensory hotspots is an unnecessary barrier.
🏃
Normalise movement and breaksBuild a culture where stepping away from a desk, taking a walk, or working standing up is entirely normal. Movement is a legitimate regulation strategy — and good for everyone's wellbeing.
🔔
Reduce unnecessary sensory demandsConsider whether team meetings, events or social activities are held in environments that are unnecessarily noisy, bright or crowded. Quieter venues benefit everyone.
📅
Communicate environmental changes in advanceOffice moves, refurbishments, changes to layout or equipment — give the whole team advance notice as standard. An unexpected environmental change can undo months of carefully built coping strategies.
🔒

A team member's condition, diagnosis or any adjustments they receive are confidential. Never disclose this information to colleagues without explicit consent from the individual.

Do

  • Frame environmental changes as general wellbeing improvements
  • Ask the individual what, if anything, they'd like colleagues to know
  • Respond to curiosity about headphone use or seating with general team norms
  • Model acceptance of different working styles without explanation

Don't

  • Explain someone's sensory needs or behaviours to colleagues
  • Draw attention to how someone sits, moves or uses equipment
  • Ask colleagues to adjust behaviour specifically for one person
  • Allow the individual to be treated as unusual for their sensory needs
